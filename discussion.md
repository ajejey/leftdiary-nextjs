The core problem you're solving
Every existing "leftist reading guide" is essentially a bibliography — a list of books with maybe a sentence about each. The Anarchist Library is an archive, not a curriculum. It assumes you already know what you're looking for.

What you're describing is fundamentally different. You want something that gives someone the feeling of having watched 40 hours of PhilosophyTube — where at the end they have a map in their head, not just a list of things they've read. Where ideas are load-bearing — each one supports the next.

That's not a reading guide. That's closer to a theory of knowledge — deciding what order reality should be explained in.

The structural problem no one has solved
Here's why this is hard and why no one has done it well:

The left doesn't have a single canon. It has competing traditions that sometimes disagree fundamentally — Marxists, anarchists, feminists, post-colonialists, ecologists. Most guides pick one tradition and pretend the others are footnotes. Or they list all traditions and give you no way to navigate between them.

PhilosophyTube's secret is that she doesn't start from ideology. She starts from a problem a human being experiences — loneliness, work, identity, power — and then shows you how different thinkers have wrestled with it. The ideology emerges from the lived experience, not the other way around.

That's the spine your guide needs.

A possible architecture
Instead of organising by tradition (Marxism → Anarchism → Feminism) or by canon (Chomsky → Graeber → hooks), organise by the questions a confused, feeling person actually asks:

Layer 1 — Something feels wrong
Why do I work so much and have so little? Why does the news feel like it's lying to me? Why does voting feel pointless? Why do some people have everything and most people have nothing?

This is where you earn trust. The reader isn't ready for theory. They need to feel seen first.

Layer 2 — Here's a language for what you're feeling
Alienation. Ideology. Class. Patriarchy. Empire. Primitive accumulation. You introduce concepts not as definitions but as tools for understanding their own life. Each concept is a new pair of glasses.

Layer 3 — Here's how these lenses connect
This is the hardest and most valuable layer. Labour exploitation and patriarchy aren't separate problems — Federici shows you how they were built together. Empire and domestic poverty aren't separate problems — they're the same system seen from different angles. This is where the map becomes a map.

Layer 4 — Here's what people have tried and what we can learn
Historical movements. What worked, what failed, and honestly why. Paris Commune, trade unions, civil rights, suffrage, decolonisation. Without this layer the guide feels like diagnosis with no path forward, which is demoralising.

Layer 5 — Here's how to keep learning on your own
Now you give them the canon. Now the Anarchist Library makes sense. Now "Bullshit Jobs" has a home in their head. The bibliography at the end is powerful. At the beginning it's just noise.

On the children's books — this is a separate and serious project
You're right that there's a gap and demand is real. But children's books are a completely different craft and shouldn't be folded into the adult guide thinking. A few things worth saying clearly:

The best political children's books don't explain ideology. They build moral intuitions — fairness, solidarity, the absurdity of arbitrary hierarchy, the value of collective action. You show a child that when the bees share the honey everyone has enough, and one bee hoarding it all makes everyone miserable including the bee. They don't need the word "capitalism." They feel the wrongness of it.

The books that have done this well — Sendak's Where the Wild Things Are in a looser sense, The Lorax, A People's History for young readers — all operate on feeling first, concept never (or much later). The ideology is in the values the story takes for granted, not in any lesson.

This is a serious craft challenge and probably deserves its own thinking session. But yes — babies and toddlers board books about sharing, mutual aid, and the dignity of work are genuinely missing from shelves.

What "inflation" actually is — stripped of the jargon
Forget the word "inflation" for a moment. Here's what actually happens in plain terms.

Imagine the government creates a hundred new rupees and spends them into the economy — pays a teacher, builds a road, whatever. Now there are a hundred more rupees in circulation than before.

If the amount of things in the economy — food, goods, services — stays the same but there is suddenly more money chasing those same things, sellers can charge more for them. Prices go up. This is all "inflation" means. More money, same amount of stuff, each unit of money buys less.

Now here is where it gets interesting. Who does this hurt? And who does it help?

Wealthy people own lots of stuff — stocks, houses, land. Their assets go up in value when prices rise. Poor people don't own much. They just get the part where their income becomes less valuable. 
American Enterprise Institute

So inflation is not neutral. It quietly takes from people who hold cash and wages — which is most working people — and gives to people who hold assets. Which is the wealthy.

But now notice something. The government could choose to spend that new money on things that increase the amount of stuff in the economy — build more houses, train more doctors, produce more food. Then there would be more money and more things, and prices might not rise at all.

The inflation question is therefore not really about money. It's about: are you producing enough real things to match the money being created? If yes — no problem. If no — prices rise and the poorest people suffer most.

So "controlling inflation" sounds technical and neutral. But what it actually means in practice is: deciding how much money gets created and who gets it first.

What tax actually does in this system — the plain version
Now here is the thing that will rewire how you think about tax forever.

MMT states the government is the monopoly issuer of its currency and therefore must spend currency into existence before any tax revenue can be collected. The government spends currency into existence and taxpayers use that currency to pay their obligations to the state. 
Wikipedia

Read that very carefully. The government spends first. Tax comes after.

This is the opposite of what we are told. We are told: the government collects your taxes, puts them in a pot, and then spends them on schools and roads. As if without your taxes there would be no money.

But that is physically impossible. Where did the first money come from? You had to get it from somewhere before you could pay tax. The government had to spend it into existence first.

So tax cannot be how the government gets money. The government creates money when it spends. Then what does tax actually do?

Paying tax cancels your liability to the government — in exactly the same way as repayment of a bank loan cancels the money that was created when the loan was made. Paying tax cancels the money. 
Tax Research Unit

Tax destroys money. It removes it from circulation. The government creates it by spending. Then removes some of it by taxing. The difference between what it created and what it removed is what stays in the economy.

So the real question tax is answering is not "how do we fund schools" — the government can always fund schools by creating money. The real question tax is answering is: how much money should exist in the economy at any given moment, and who should have it?
